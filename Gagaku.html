II. Gagaku in Medieval Japanese Religion

The use of music to express the divine and promote spiritual awakening

Scene from the Life of the Buddha - See more from the MET collection.

Gagaku is a form of traditional Japanese court music that has been closely associated with Japanese religion and culture for over a thousand years. gagaku originated in China and was introduced to Japan during the Nara period (710-794 CE). Over time, the music evolved into a unique Japanese form that incorporated elements of both indigenous and foreign musical traditions.

During the medieval period, gagaku became closely associated with Buddhism, particularly the Tendai and Shingon sects. Gagaku music was performed at religious ceremonies and festivals, where it was believed to have a transformative effect on the participants.

One example of gagaku's significance in Japanese Buddhist visual culture can be seen (once again) in the Amida Triad of the Kamakura period, which depicts the Amida Buddha surrounded by musicians playing gagaku instruments. The musicians are shown in ornate costumes and masks, emphasizing the theatricality and ritualistic nature of the performance.

Odaiko

The Odaiko is a colossal drum with a commanding presence. With its thunderous sound and imposing size, the Odaiko represents power, reverence, and the ability to invoke a deep sense of spirituality.
