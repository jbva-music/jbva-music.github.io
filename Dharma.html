III. The Dharma of Music

Expressing Buddhist concepts through sound

The Essentials of Rebirth in the Pure Land (Ojōyōshū) - See more from the MET collection.

Music has played a crucial role in expressing the Buddhist concept of emptiness, or shunyata, which emphasizes the impermanence and interdependence of all things.

Music in Japanese Buddhism is seen as a way of representing the flux and impermanence of reality, while also expressing the interconnectedness of all things. This is reflected in the use of instruments like the shakuhachi flute, which is used to create a sense of emptiness and impermanence through its use of breath and silence.

Furthermore, music in Japanese Buddhism can also be seen as a way of expressing the concept of non-duality, or the idea that all phenomena are fundamentally interconnected and inseparable. This is exemplified in the use of the sho, a mouth organ with multiple pipes that create a complex, layered sound that is intended to symbolize the interconnectedness of all things.

Shakuhachi Flute

Known for its haunting and meditative tones, the Shakuhachi has been used by Zen monks as a tool for deep introspection, mindfulness, and as a means of expressing the indescribable nature of existence. Its breathy tone and versatile range evoke a sense of tranquility, inviting listeners to embark on a contemplative journey.
