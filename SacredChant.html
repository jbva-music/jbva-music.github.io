I. Sacred Chant in Medieval Japan

An exploration of the role of chant in Japanese Buddhist practice

Descent and Return of Amida to Western Paradise with a Believer's Soul (Gōshō mandara) - See more from the MET collection.

Sacred chant was a crucial aspect of music in Japanese Buddhist visual culture during the medieval period. In his article "Medieval Sacred Chant: from Japan to Portugal," Manuel Pedro Ferreira explores the history of sacred chant in Japan and its influence on music in other parts of the world. The earliest known form of sacred chant in Japan was known as shomyo, which was introduced from China in the 6th century. Over time, shomyo evolved into many different styles, each associated with a particular sect of Buddhism.

One of the most significant developments in sacred chant during the medieval period was the emergence of the Tendai sect's Shingon-shu style. This style of chanting was characterized by its use of microtonal intervals and complex rhythms, which were believed to have a transformative effect on the listener. The Shingon-shu style also incorporated elements of vocal improvisation, allowing performers to express their individual interpretations of the text.

The importance of sacred chant in medieval Japan can be seen in many examples of Buddhist art from the period. For instance, the Heian-period Lotus Sutra scroll depicts the Bodhisattva Manjusri singing the sutra to a group of monks, while the Amida Triad of the Kamakura period shows the Amida Buddha accompanied by musicians playing the shomyo chant.

Biwa & Koto Instruments

The biwa is a pear-shaped, plucked string instrument with a rich, resonant sound and a history spanning centuries in Japan. The koto, on the other hand, is a large zither-like instrument with 13 strings stretched across a rectangular wooden body. Known for its delicate, melodic tones, the koto is considered one of the most iconic traditional instruments in Japan, played by plucking the strings with the fingers and adjusting pitch using movable bridges.
